# Alicia-Love-Artificial-Intelligence-Hist-19879-ITAI-1370

Houston City College Spring 2026 - Portfolio Final Exam

The story of Artificial Intelligence is, at its core, a story about human ambition, the relentless pursuit of building machines that think, learn, and communicate. It began not with silicon chips or data centers, but with a thought experiment. In 1950, British mathematician Alan Turing published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," posing the provocative question: "Can machines think?" That single question ignited a field. Six years later, in the summer of 1956, a landmark workshop at Dartmouth College,convened by John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester, formally introduced the world to a new discipline: Artificial Intelligence. The pioneers gathered there believed machines as intelligent as humans could exist within a generation. They were not entirely wrong. They were only early.

The decades that followed were marked by extraordinary promise and sobering setbacks. Early successes in natural language processing, game-playing systems, and symbolic reasoning gave way to the first "AI winter" of the 1970s, when the sheer complexity of human intelligence far exceeded what researchers had anticipated. The 1980s brought a revival through expert systems, and it was here that a name I know well entered the story. AT&T's own Bell Laboratories, through its Adaptive Systems Research Department (founded in 1984 in Holmdel, New Jersey), became a quiet but profound contributor to artificial neural network research for computer vision. Bell Labs had already given the world the transistor (1947), the UNIX operating system (1969),the foundation upon which the internet was built, and the first mobile telephone call. These were not merely telecommunications milestones; they were the infrastructure upon which the digital age, and ultimately AI itself, was constructed. The 1990s brought IBM's Deep Blue defeating world chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997. The 2000s saw machine learning mature quietly in research labs. Then the GPU revolution of the 2010s gave deep learning the computational power it had always required, and everything accelerated. AlexNet, AlphaGo, GPT-3, and by 2026, generative AI woven into the fabric of daily life.

I witnessed much of this arc from a front-row seat.

For 26 years, I built my career at AT&T in Information Technology, serving as a Communication Consultant in a Project Management role. I experienced firsthand the seismic technological shifts that transformed American telecommunications: the transition from analog to digital networks, the deployment of fiber optic infrastructure, the enterprise introduction of cloud-based services that AT&T pioneered, and the gradual migration of physical, on-premises systems into what the industry now calls legacy services — the foundational architecture upon which today's cloud platforms stand. Every project I managed was a chapter in that larger story: the digitization of how America communicates. I did not fully recognize it at the time, but I was working at the precise convergence of telecommunications, computing, and the earliest practical applications of intelligent systems.

When I retired after 26 years, I carried that knowledge forward, not as a relic of the past, but as the foundation for what comes next.

I enrolled at Houston City College in the Spring 2026 semester for ITAI-1370: Artificial Intelligence History — not as a passive student, but as a practitioner returning to formalize two and a half decades of lived technological experience and expand into the frontier of what lies ahead. This course fulfills a mandatory requirement for the Associate of Applied Science (AAS) degree and represents the deliberate first step on a path toward a Bachelor of Applied Technology (BAT) in Artificial Intelligence. After a career spent at the intersection of technology and human communication, the very intersection where AI now operates, pursuing formal study in the field defining the next era of civilization is not a departure from who I am. It is the natural and inevitable next chapter.

This portfolio documents that chapter: the modules studied, the assignments completed, the concepts mastered, and the professional identity being rebuilt, at the intersection of a rich career and an extraordinary future.
